Master's Thesis: Fuel Prices and EV Demand in New Zealand

BSNS9000 Industry Research Project examining how fuel prices, policy settings, and behavioural factors shape electric and hybrid vehicle adoption in New Zealand.

Type: Master's Thesis
Scale: 89-page report • N=103 survey
Methods: Regression • Granger Causality • ARIMA • TCO trial
Read Full Thesis (PDF)

Research Question

Do rising fuel prices increase EV adoption in New Zealand, and what barriers prevent long-term transition despite high consumer price sensitivity?

Why This Project Matters

The thesis connects quantitative analysis with a current policy and consumer-behaviour problem: whether fuel-price pressure alone is enough to shift adoption, or whether policy certainty and perceived ownership economics matter more.

Methodology

  • 15-year panel analysis (2010-2024) across New Zealand, Norway, China, and the US
  • Primary consumer survey with 103 New Zealand respondents
  • Three-month real-world ownership trial using a 2011 Nissan Leaf
  • Quantitative modeling using elasticity analysis, Granger causality, and ARIMA forecasting

Key Findings

  • New Zealand shows strong fuel-price sensitivity in uncontrolled adoption models
  • Policy instability suppresses adoption even when fuel prices remain high
  • Removal of incentives created a major registration drop despite continued fuel pressure
  • Perception-reality gap in ownership costs remains a major behavioural barrier

Policy Contribution

The thesis proposes a staged, revenue-neutral 7-step policy plan to scale electrified vehicle adoption, reduce fuel import dependency, and align transport with long-term emissions reduction targets.

What This Demonstrates

This project demonstrates end-to-end research design, econometric analysis, survey design, synthesis of quantitative and qualitative evidence, and the ability to translate findings into practical policy recommendations.
